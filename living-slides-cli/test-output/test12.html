Why Distribute?03

Three forcing functions drive every distribution decision

Scale

No single machine can hold petabytes of data or handle six-figure QPS.

Availability

Hardware fails. Software crashes. Distribute to survive any single failure.

Locality

Users on five continents need sub-100ms responses. Bring data closer.

Partition Tolerance07

When the network splits, what does your system do?

  • Quorum reads and writes (R + W > N) for tunable consistency.
  • Hinted handoff: store missed writes for absent replicas.
  • Anti-entropy with Merkle trees to reconcile divergent state.
  • Read repair on the hot path; sync repair in the background.
  • Vector clocks or hybrid logical clocks for ordering.

Database Sharding10

Shard by access pattern, never by what feels natural

Hash sharding

Even distribution. Range queries become scatter-gather.

Range sharding

Efficient scans. Risk of hot shards on time-series data.

Directory sharding

Maximum flexibility, cost of an extra lookup hop.

War Stories16

Three incidents that taught us more than any textbook

  • Thundering herd: cache stampede after a 30-second outage took down the DB.
  • Cascading retries: client retry storms multiplied load 8x during partial failure.
  • Clock drift: a 200ms NTP skew silently corrupted ordering for 6 hours.

Best Practices17

Six rules we now write into every design doc

  • Make every operation idempotent by default.
  • Set deadlines on every RPC, no exceptions.
  • Circuit-break dependencies that miss SLOs.
  • Bound retries with exponential backoff and jitter.
  • Embrace backpressure rather than queuing forever.
  • Test failure scenarios with chaos engineering, weekly.

Takeaways18

Three things to remember tomorrow morning

1

Pick consistency last

Start with the weakest model and strengthen only when business demands it.

2

Design for failure

Networks partition. Disks corrupt. Plan for the worst case as the default.

3

Measure everything

If a metric does not exist, the behavior does not exist. Instrument first.
